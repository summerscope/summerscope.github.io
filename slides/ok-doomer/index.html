Let's refocus

So instead of giving this topic more oxygen, let's spend the rest of our time exploring sensible and relatively cheap and easy design choices we can make to reduce risk and improve AI safety.

Broad-sense baseline

  • A calculation
  • Computer logic
  • Encoded business rules

What do I mean by baseline? The simplest way you can imagine to solve the problem

Plan for take-down

What do you do with catastrophic failure cases? How can you turn the model off, once it's in production? See: andon cord in toyota manufacturing

Maintenance mode

Have a page ready to show users if you have to take your system out of production, deploy breaking changes, etc

Graceful degradation

Revert to your baseline

Your baseline is like your back-up power generator. It's not as nice as the normal power source, but better than going into full black-out mode.

System roll-back

  1. Version control on your model and training data?
  2. Doing careful release management?
  3. If yes to #1 and preferably #2, you could revert to a previous working state, rather than taking it down
Don't try this if you're still getting the basics in place. In case of a catastrophic failure no one will be doing their best thinking, so a simpler take-down would be better.

ML-specific design

This is a quick overview of design patterns I see emerging and/or needed, which are specific to tech systems being powered by a machine learning model

Label the machine

Let people know if they're talking to a person or a bot. Make it clear what's under the hood.

Expose machine confidence

All predictions are not created equal. Some are right on a classification boundary. This is important user-facing information.

Phone a (human) friend

Consider if it makes sense in application for every single use-case to output a decision, classification, or prediction. Identify uncertainty levels where automation is unacceptable, and kick it over to a human reviewer.

Explain machine decisions

understand how and why the system made the decision about us that it did.

Supply receipts

Another simple concept which often goes missing in the wash - how can you trace back precisely that interaction, that data point, that user, and that (state of the) model?

Provide a decision record

  • Unique ID
  • Logs
  • Version info
Notes

Decision recourse

What do system operators need to make a grounded assessment of the decision?

In order to feel truly fair, we need Decision recourse - to know what we could do differently to change the outcome. If the answer is "be born a different gender", or "have a wealthy family" - these aren't helpful. Think of an admin view, or admin dashboard. What can you expose to them to help them assess the correctness of the decision?

Start with fewer people

A huge way to reduce risk is to reduce the number of potential failure cases and other issues with a careful roll-out

Trial run(s)

  • Select the group carefully
  • Communicate with them intensively
  • Assume your system will fail
This could be called a beta, or whatever you like. Try your system with a very limited and carefully selected group. Start with the assumption that it won't work perfectly for everyone, and improve iteratively.

Phased roll-out

  • Break your audience up into chunks
  • Communicate with them intensively
  • Assume your system will fail
When you're ready to go into production, no need to flip the switch for all users right away. Roll out in smaller groups, on a pre-planned schedule. Try to allow time to recover from unexpected bugs and failure modes.

Feature flags

Hide experimental features in prod
(until they're ready to go-live)

Once you're in prod again you don't have to think of feature deployment and roll-out as all or nothing. You can hide features behind feature-flags while you test with select users, and you could turn-off the feature flag in a staged way rather than for all users.

Feedback loops

Timely, contextual feedback loops. This is a fancy way to say - please, please, talk to your users! Before you deploy, during beta testing, in prod.

Consider all users of your system

  • Customer users (pay for the system)
  • Operator users (admins)
  • Training users (data labelling)
  • Classified users (system classifies or predicts)
  • End users (native or web apps)
Unfortunately one of the realities of machine learning is the cast of characters is much bigger than a standard software system. If you involve a human in the building, deployment, or use of your system -- the onus is on you to consider their needs and design accordingly.

Front-load your feedback efforts

You should anticipate feedback at all stages of the software lifecycle, but it's sensible to put significant effort earlier in the lead-up to deployment.

Talk to users directly

  • Create a shared slack, discord, group chat
  • Embed users in your team, or vice-versa
  • Stay curious
  • Listen to all signals - verbal and non-verbal
  • Ask about pain points and successes
  • Don't ask for design or solutions
ML engineers and data scientists SHOULD NOT be sheltered from user feedback. You don't want to forget that there are real folks on the other side of your algo, and the more real they are to you, the better you can design for them.

Operationalising feedback

  • Model re-training
  • Set thresholds for user feedback
User feedback can turn into labeled data for re-training. Let folks change their mind, and preferences. If you're not doing continuous learning, plan for frequent re-training. Establish a baseline of feedback - perhaps sentiment analysis to identify positive or negative, if not other metric. Set thresholds of quality - if breaching these, set up alerts in slack, pager duty etc - let your engineers know that something has gone wrong.

A quick recap

  • Start with a baseline
  • Plan for take-down
  • ML-specific design
  • Supply receipts
  • Start with fewer people
  • Feedback loops
