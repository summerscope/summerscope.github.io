A tooling gap

Sweet Summer Child Score

What is it?

  • A self-assessment quiz which gives you a risk score
  • Scans for harms to people and communities by a socio-technical system
  • Risk reduction suggestions alongside the score

How can I take the quiz?

  1. Online in the web app
  2. Locally in CLI (python)
  3. Locally in CLI or RStudio (r)
  4. Locally in CLI (rust)

Three main sections

  1. Cohort scan
  2. System scan
  3. Maslov's hierarchy of needs

The three sections are each worth roughly 1/3 of the overall score

Multiplier

Adjusts the score to reflect the number of people who could be impacted

Basically it draws on the intuition that a system that could harm 10 people should be ranked as less risky than a system that could harm 1,000 or 100,000.
Cohort size Multiplier
1-100 0.52
101-1,000 0.75
1,000-10,000 0.85
10,000-100,000 0.95
100,000-1,000,000 0.96
1,000,000-10,000,000 0.98
10 million + 1.0

How is the score calculated?

  • Start with 99 points (highest possible score)
  • Add points from each section
  • (SumPoints) x multiplier
  • 99 - (AdjustedSum)
  • The lower your final score, the higher the risk assessment

#1 Cohort scan

Thinking about the folks who could be negatively impacted by the system
Not limited to end users

A socio-technical system

  • Folks the system makes classifications or predictions about
  • Folks who train the system
  • Folks who operate the system
  • Folks who pay for the system
  • End users of the system
Why not scan for minority idendities?
We focus on the disadvantages usually experienced by these populations rather than the demographics of disadvantage. We don't want to contribute to the conflation of minority identities and harmful impacts.

Case study

Insurance app - Fraud detection

  • Features overlap 'good' and 'bad' actors
  • 🕵️ VPN use 👉 high tech confidence
  • ❌ No 2FA 👉 low tech confidence
Fraud detection algorithms can be fitted on features that correspond with high technology confidence (VPN use) or low technology confidence (2FA). Doing a cohort scan flags their existence. Technologists need to work out how to pragmatically assess the signals available to them, but also to make it easy for misclassified customers to right themselves easily and bake in "false positive pathway" as a key part of the user journey.

#1 Cohort Scan

Example questions

  • Is your target cohort economically vulnerable?
    Compare to a baseline of average earning rates for region

  • Is your target cohort over-policed or over-surveilled?
    Consider if they are more likely to be targeted by police, social services or surveillance systems

#2 System scan

Implementation of the system in it's context.
Your system as it interacts with the world.
This is the most powerful section!

Why?

Options we have to observe and mitigate risk

Case study

Film recommender
Outbreak/Contagion/Pandemic

  • Data drift in production
  • Black swan events
  • Can you take it down?
Say you're working on a Netflix-like movie recommendation system and it uses a news scanning model to identify current news topics. Then the world gets hit with another COVID like event, and suddenly all it recommends is virus movies and your user base is not super happy about it. Our system scan asks us questions like "can you take it down from production easily" and that gives us a clue into the reputational costs at stake.

#2 System Scan

Example questions

  • Can you turn your decision system off instantly if something goes wrong?
    One click take down? Write and deploy code?

  • Does your system always output a classification, prediction, or decision?

  • Can your target cohort request a decision review?

#3 Maslov's hierarchy of needs

Slightly updated for the modern era
Compares the likelihood of harm with no intervention (if your system didn't exist) to the likelihood of this harm occurring as a result of your intervention.
This section is tricky because we don't have perfect visibility of all our system's impacts & flow-on effects.

(Trust your gut)

Case study

Time series
Hospital Staffing

  • Hospital cost savings vs.
  • Patient's right to care
Time series modelling to predict the variable staffing needs of a hospital, and this is directly hooked into the procurement arm. In a severe failure mode where you're wildly understaffed and there's no mechanism to observe and course correct, you directly negatively impacting the experience or life expectancy of patients in the hospital.

#3 Maslov's
Hierarchy
of Needs

Example questions

  • Physiological needs
    Probability that your automated decision system will directly, negatively impact your target cohort's Physiological needs
    (Air, water, food, shelter, sleep, reproduction)

  • Esteem needs
    Probability that your automated decision system will directly, negatively impact your target cohort's Esteem needs
    (Respect, self-esteem, status, recognition, strength, freedom)
