In others, not so much...

especially verticals where there are captive markets, like medicine, or govt.

so here it is...

a whirlwind tour of obstacles, challenges, and cognitive biases you may encounter when attempting to disrupt

in the enterprise

most 'important' problems are hard to tackle because they're tangled up inside large, beaurocratic systems. Think climate change. Think our broken economic systems, the decline of the middle class, or politics. This is why innovation for enterprise matters: we need to learn how to tackle these problems; break them down into small manageble chunks, and not throw them in the 'too-hard basket'.

the tender process

Y SO SRS??? Problems with tenders: time & resource intensive. written to favor an existing vendor's product. too prescriptive: define the solution & required technology, not the problem. Onerous requirements that disadvantage small or new vendors (length of time in business, required support, etc)

first date, or .... ?

Image attribution

As a potential vendor, you just want to rock up to a first date, have a drink, feel out the chemistry. Tenders are like - we're getting married, and here's the prenup, sign now!

anti-tender

impact investing is a growing segment

Networking! Get on their radar before they write the tender. Partner with a large company. Gov't grants & subsidies. Purchasing budget which falls below tender threshold?

the sales cycle

Google research on hospital purchasing decisions

Time to select technology vendors and sign a contract can be ridiculous! think anything from 6 months to 2 years. Lots of stakeholders. Traditionally discover vendors at trade shows - this is changing slowly.

 

reimagine the sales cycle

Plan for it - you need to have capital to survive. Use this time to improve your product. Advocate for change with whoever will listen. Steal customer acquisition models from retail. i.e. an offer that's so low risk or so appealing they can't refuse. 3 months trial, fully integrated, free. Guaranteed XX% improvement in YY% metric or it's free.

your end users !== your clients

This chestnut basically negates all the standard measures of customer engagement and satisfaction. Also means that there can be latent resentment against top-down technology decisions - before your product has had a chance.

make user success metrics part of the deliverable

over 70% of enterprise projects fail
because of a lack of user acceptance

Failing to measure and assess standard user is simply short sighted arrogance on the POV of large beaurocracies. Expose how much these success measures will improve efficiency, save time and money on training, adoption and maintenance.

risk aversion theatre

Taking off shoes in the airport security screening line. In hospitals this is called the 'swiss cheese method'. The reason it's dangerous as well as annoying is when it prevents people from actually doing their work (re nurse & 8 page form)

call bulshit when you see it

We have to help expose the risks inherent here. Technology is good at measuring tasks, so work out how to identify metrics that make these failures of logic blindingly obvious. If you can do it for page engagement and conversion buttons, you can do it for real world problems too!

status quo bias

"stick with the devil you know" This is the charming bias responsible for enterprise systems thinking that it's safer from a systems vulnerability POV to run a restricted windows XP environment with IE7 than it is to upgrade and run OS updates. This is still a thing. In 2016.

rage against the status quo

Is it arrogant to think that as a single sys admin you can do a better job of patching vulnerabilities than say the entire team at windows, or apple? If so, we gotta say so.

UI complexity perceived as functionality & value

we tend to think that greater functionality means greater value
This is a bias known to scientists, mathemeticians, even the odd programmer. The equating of design simplicity as surface 'gloss' or pretty - without understanding the deep underpinning work which makes products usable.

time for a UX smackdown

Time to get our UX and product design advocates front & center! We're excellent at arguing for the importance of good UX, given the opportunity.

price anchoring

the human tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information offered when making decisions
 
with pricing, context matters
whereby competitor pricing makes your pricing look so cheap as to be fake or unfeasible.
