Let's travel to Athens...

I'd like to invite you to go on a journey with me... We're going back in time to the end of the 4th century BC, in ancient Athens. Direct democracy is in full swing. Any citizen could speak to the assembly and vote on decisions by simply holding up their hands. The majority won the day.

Plato v. Sophists

Amongst the thinkers of the day, there's a bit of academic cat-fighting happening. Teachers, named sophists, who travel around greece providing wisdom for pay, have earned the ire of Plato. The fees they charge mean that the poorer citizens miss out, and the techniques they teach focus on style over substance...

Sophists might be considered the
dark-pattern designers of their day

The danger plato was most worried about was the sophists bamboozling ordinary people with language - convincing them to vote for things against their best interests.

In politics we presume that everyone who knows how to get votes knows how to administer a city or a state. When we are ill... we do not ask for the handsomest physician, or the most
eloquent one.

And the Greeks were all like, sick burn, Plato!!

Aristotle v. Plato

And then along comes Aristotle, Plato's prize student. While he agreed with Plato about the shallowness and inherent danger of the sophists, he ultimately turned against his mentor's rejection of rhetoric to embrace the study and techniques of persuasion. Aristotle's work, Rhetoric, is an expansive study of the art of persuasion, particularly as it is directed towards a non-academic public.

Nevertheless, Rhetoric is useful, because the true and the just are naturally superior to their opposites, so that, if decisions are improperly made, they must owe their defeat to their own advocates; which is reprehensible.

And everybody was like, damn, 'siker burn, Aristotle!'

So what does all this
have to do with UI?

ok so cool story, bro. but you're probably wondering what can these dusty greek philosophers teach us about designing computer interfaces today...

Scalability

These guys - the sophists, Plato, and Aristotle after him (and then later in Rome Cicero, and lots of folks in between) - they're all concerned with how to scale the process of convincing someone to do something. The ultimate goal of giving a speech is to persuade, scalably.
Now, what other applications can we think of that might be concerned with scaling a conversation or automating an interaction?

I know!

Everyone! Scaled conversations are the basis of pretty much all modern UIs! For the purposes of this talk we'll focus on the domain of conversational UI - chatbots, personal assistants, ambiant IOT devices - applications for natural language recognition and AI. It's worth noting that nearly all user interfaces employ some form of rhetoric- and if you expand your definition of 'persuasion' to 'engagement' then basically anywhere you have a conversion metric, you have rhetoric.

Let's steal invent some new heuristics!

I'd like to suggest that some of the failings of these experiences can be solved with better and more creative design thinking, rather than putting all the burden on smarter AI. So I propose we take the work these very smart academics were doing, nearly 2,500 years ago, to define some new heuristics for conversational interfaces.

The 5 Cannons of Rhetoric

Cicero in ancient Rome

Invention - finding something to say - what is said Arrangement - the order of speech or writing Style - the artful expression of ideas Memory - remembering the speech Delivery - the tone, speed, inflection - how it's delivered

Invention, for example

Thinking creatively about the conversation, provide meaning and richness to the interaction. Think about good speaches, good comedy, good scripts- they are rarely one-track and entirely predictable - what makes them great is their ability to meander but still bring it all together at the end.

Style, for example

This bus arrivals app is in 'singlish' and the tone of voice is in the singapore / english creole. Aside from establishing ethos, it provides a playful and creative aspect to what would otherwise be the dull work of waiting for a bus.

Memory, for example

Memory was traditionally what the speaker had in their brain. Now, aside from knowing the script, our UIs can have the context of previous conversations, remembering and reasoning based on past requests - to make the experience richer. Conversely, memory might be re-pitched as 'what the interface agrees to forget', i.e. personal details, previous transactions you don't want stored.

Delivery, for example

I used this example as it's easy to visualise, but this can also be applied to pure conversation. The speed of the response or reply, the timing of the words or design elements, important pauses, rapid-fire statements: all tools at our disposal in this domain.

To return to the beginning... the moral conundrum which aristotle struggled; the ethics and objectives behind rhetoric, applies equally to computer interfaces. if we take it as given that technology is inherently agnostic then our interfaces as rhetors are to blame for the bad outcomes as well as the good.

A challenge and an opportunity

I see the surge of conversational UI as a way to achieve deeper and more thoughtful interactions. It's the perfect opportunity to tackle a long-standing problem of UI - designing for both power users and noobs. This style of interface can be deep or shallow: bounded only by the curiosity of the user. It's up to us to decide whether we want the web to be just a series of shopfronts or to fulfil its original vision as a matrix of information, communication and knowledge.
