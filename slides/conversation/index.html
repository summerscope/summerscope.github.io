Let's travel to Athens...

I'd like to invite you to go on a journey with me... We're going back in time to the end of the 4th century BC, in ancient Athens. Direct democracy is in full swing. Any citizen could speak to the assembly and vote on decisions by simply holding up their hands. The majority won the day.

Plato v. Sophists

Amongst the thinkers of the day, there's a bit of academic cat-fighting happening. Teachers, named sophists, who travel around greece teaching public speaking have earned the ire of Plato. Charging fees = poorer citizens miss out, and the techniques they teach focus on style over substance..."half truthes and fine sounded meaninglessness"

Sophists might be considered the
dark-pattern designers of their day

The danger plato was most worried about was the sophists bamboozling ordinary people with language - convincing them to vote for things against their best interests.

In politics we presume that everyone who knows how to get votes knows how to administer a city or a state. When we are ill... we do not ask for the handsomest physician, or the most eloquent one.

Aristotle v. Plato

And then along comes Aristotle, Plato's prize student. While he agreed with Plato about the shallowness and inherent danger of the sophists, he ultimately rejected his mentor's view. Aristotle's work, Rhetoric, is an expansive study of the art of persuasion particularly as it is directed towards a non-academic public.

Nevertheless, Rhetoric is useful, because the true and the just are naturally superior to their opposites, so that, if decisions are improperly made, they must owe their defeat to their own advocates; which is reprehensible.

Scalability

These guys - the sophists, Plato, and Aristotle after him (and then later in Rome Cicero, and lots of folks in between) - they're all concerned with how to scale the process of convincing someone to do something. The ultimate goal of giving a speech is to persuade, scalably.
Can we think of any other domains that might be concerned with scaling a conversation or automating an interaction?

I know!

Nearly all user interfaces! for example, chatbots, personal assistants, smarthome & IOT, e-commerce, robo-advice, e-health. For the purposes of this talk we'll focus on conversational UI. If you expand your definition of user 'conversion' to 'engagement' then basically anywhere you have a conversion metric, you have rhetoric.

The 5 Cannons of Rhetoric

Cicero in ancient Rome

Invention

Arrangement

Style

Memory

Delivery

Invention - finding something to say
Arrangement - the order of speech
Style - the artful expression of ideas
Memory - remembering the speech
Delivery - the tone, speed, inflection

Invention, for example

Thinking creatively about the conversation, provide meaning and richness to the interaction. Think about good speeches, good comedy, good scripts- they are rarely one-track and entirely predictable. what makes them great is their ability to meander but still bring it all together at the end.

Style, for example

Style = vocabulary used, 'design' of the language. This app 'singlish' - tone of voice is the singapore / english creole. Aside from establishing ethos, it provides a playful and creative aspect to what would otherwise be the dull work of waiting for a bus.

Memory, for example

Memory traditionally was recall ability. Now our UIs can have the context of previous conversations, remembering and reasoning based on past requests - to make the experience richer. Conversely, memory might be re-imagined as 'what the interface agrees to forget', i.e. personal details, previous transactions you don't want stored.

Delivery, for example

The speed of the response or reply, the timing of the words or design elements, important pauses, tone, emphasis, inflection: all tools at our disposal in this domain.
I used this example as it's easy to visualise, but this can also be applied to pure conversation.

To return to the beginning... the moral conundrum which aristotle struggled with; the ethics and objectives behind rhetoric, applies equally to computer interfaces. if we take it as given that technology is inherently agnostic then our interfaces, as rhetors, are to blame for the bad outcomes as well as the good.

A challenge and an opportunity

Surge of conversational UI = way to achieve deeper interactions. Opportunity to tackle a long-standing problem of UI: designing for both power users and noobs. Conv. UI can be deep or shallow: bounded only by the curiosity of the user. Up to us: Web = shopfronts; or fulfil its original vision as a matrix of information, communication and knowledge.
